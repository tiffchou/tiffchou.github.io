ENTRY 5: SNOQUALMIE PASS TO WHITE PASS
Days 19 - 23 | July 22 - 26 | Mile 259.5 - 357.7 | 98.2 miles

You’d think thru-hikers would be eager to start walking, seeing as we’re doing it most hours of each day. Not always so. In fact, I’d say thru-hikers are some of the most lazy people when we’re in town. I was “ready” to leave Snoqualmie Pass around 8:00 AM, judging by the timestamps on my photos. Didn’t actually leave until noon, and there really isn’t anything to do around Snoqualmie Pass. We’re really good at finding reasons to postpone starting the day. (Most of these reasons usually revolve around town food.)


The days were getting warmer, but water wasn't a problem at all

The first night out of Snoqualmie Pass was one of the days our group got split up, and it might have been a good thing. When Oli and I reached the campsite around the same time that night, we realized the site we had all agreed to meet up at was hilariously tiny. We spent several minutes standing there, trying to figure out the best way to get all 5 of our tents into this ridiculously-sized site.

A little while later, Tyce showed up, and we were able to get all 3 of our tents in there, although we sort of had to commit to not leaving in the middle of the night to pee because we’d trip all over each others’ guylines or stakes. We waited until it got dark for TL and Yahtzee to show up, but they never did. Again, not sure that was a bad thing that night because even looking at the photos now long after the fact, I’m not sure how we’d fit their tents into this spot.


Getting really cuddly and cozy at this tiny campsite

We had a couple of firsts in this section. We started getting cell service on the trail for the first time. It was actually kind of overwhelming after basically being in the middle of nowhere for a couple weeks. Oh, and Oli had Nutella from the jar with a spoon for the first time.


Trail in the burn area

One of the sections we walked through was the Falls Creek burn area. According to an informational sign, a fire in 1988 destroyed over 3000 acres of forest. It was a really interesting part of the trail to walk through after all of the rocky passes and lush forest of the northern half of Washington, since all the trees were burned down but there was new growth coming in.


Informational sign about the Falls Creek Fire

We had more mornings of heavy condensation and dew, so I was spending lunch time sitting around drying all of my stuff out. The days were pretty warm and sunny, so this wasn’t hard. On one of these days, I made it to the Mike Urich cabin for lunch, and there were plenty of other hikers sitting outside in the meadow next to it airing their stuff out as well. It’s always a lot of fun to sit around with hikers every so often, especially since I spent so much time in my own head while actually hiking.


Mike Urich Cabin

We passed through more sections that had been burned down, and as we continued on, there was more and more lupine everywhere, which made for some very pretty scenery.


All the lupine was a nice change in scenery

This is where we started encountering the worst mosquitoes. God, I hate mosquitoes. When I got to Snoqualmie Pass, I went online and ordered this stylish piece of incredibly practical outdoor gear so I could be prepared for the onslaught coming around White Pass. We had heard stories of how terrible they were going to get around there, and I was going to be prepared.


Imagine Mount St. Helens like 26000x bigger than it looks here and that's how big it actually looked to me

I did my first 25-mile day here! And we started getting glimpses of Mount Adams and Mount St. Helens, both of which I had previously stood at the summit of (well, for St. Helens, the crater rim). And Rainier was getting closer and closer.


Mount Rainier, just getting bigger by the mile

When we got to White Pass, we all decided to split a room at the Inn next to the Kracker Barrel, and really relax. We hitched out to Packwood, kind of nearby, to get some groceries at the real grocery store they had there (there really isn’t much at White Pass) because the room at the Inn had a full kitchen, and we were craving real food. We had to get 2 separate hitches because the first car couldn’t fit all of us, and the hitch that gave Oli and me a ride took us to their home right next to the grocery store and gave us some freshly-laid eggs from their own chickens. Seriously, I cannot tell you how amazing some trail angels are!

Jawad decided to drive over since I was able to reach White Pass on a weekend, and it’s only a few hours drive from Seattle, so he stayed with us at the Inn. It was so nice to just chill for almost an entire day.


Trailhead bulletin at White Pass

We spent most of the following day hanging out with other hikers in and around the Kracker Barrel. I spoke to one hiker who was currently hiking SOBO, although she had been jumping around the trail since February. She said she was getting off trail once she got to Cascade Locks because she had run out of money, even though there were several parts of the trail she didn't get to hit. It was definitely interesting to talk to someone whose hike was so severely affected by the snow in the Sierra, and it made me grateful that I had the flexibility in my plans to switch from a NOBO hike to a SOBO hike so I could avoid the snow.

I spoke to another hiker, Mavrik, who would eventually end up giving me a lot of great advice about the Arizona Trail in early 2020, since that was another hike I was going to take on (but COVID-19 ended up interfering with that thru-hike attempt). And we ran into Dan/Legs again, which was great because we were able to really properly thank him for helping us on the Canadian border tag with some painkillers, since we didn't get to see him after the border tag to say thanks again.

We didn't leave White Pass until around 4:00 PM, which at that point was kind of becoming par for the course on town days. It's just so hard to leave civilzation!
